From the sacred enclosure to ritual destruction — New Ireland, Papua New Guinea

I
Act I · Fabrication
The Sacred Block
Sculptor's workshop at Lainaru, near Lugagun, New Ireland. Photo: P. Gerhard Peekel, Anthropos XXIV, 1929.
Act I · Stage 1 of 4

The sculptor enters the maradan

The making of a malanggan begins in secrecy. The sculptor works within an enclosed space — the maradan — screened from the community. His activity is simultaneously a craft act and a sacred act: the opening of the wood to the motifs commissioned by the right-holder.

The wood most used is alstonia (Alstonia scholaris, known locally as milkwood for its milky sap) — light, soft, adapted to the intricate openwork patterns that give the malanggan its characteristic appearance: a figure suspended in a cage of interlacing forms. Multiple sculptures may be fabricated simultaneously for the same ceremony, each carrying the rights of a different clan or lineage.

The sculptor does not invent. He actualises a mental prototype transmitted through rights. His technical mastery and his ritual knowledge are inseparable — one without the other is insufficient.

The double constraint

Technical constraint (the grain of the wood, the structural logic of the openwork) and ritual constraint (the specific motifs belonging to the commissioning clan) are inseparable. The sculptor has no freedom of invention — and yet the result achieves a formal complexity that no unconstrained process could have reached. The constraint is the instrument.

Act II · Stage 2 of 4 · Hover the coloured zones

Reading the surface

The sculpture receives its skin in two operations: first the openwork carving exposes the skeletal structure of motifs; then painted patterns invest every surface. The result is a dense superimposition of visual vocabularies — each zone carrying a specific meaning, a specific right, a specific social obligation.

Public and secret readings · Derlon 1997

The surface carries two simultaneous readings: motifs legible to all community members, and motifs whose meaning is accessible only to initiates and right-holders. The same surface is simultaneously a public statement and a restricted text. The European eye receives only the first layer.

III
Act III · Ceremony
The Revelation
Malanggan display house at Medina, New Ireland. Photo: Felix Speiser. Museum für Völkerkunde, Basel.
Act III · Stage 3 of 4

The soul's passage

The completed malanggan is installed in a temporary exhibition house alongside other effigies. The assembled figures represent multiple clans, multiple lineages, multiple sets of rights. Chanting and the dances of the Tatanua mask surround the display for several days.

The sculpture serves as a temporary receptacle for the soul of the deceased. During the ceremony, the spirit makes its final passage from the world of the living to the world of the ancestors. The malanggan is neither portrait nor memorial. It is a vehicle.

At this moment, a specific individual receives the right to commission the next malanggan of each type displayed — the mental prototype as a binding social obligation, carried invisibly until the next death requires its actualisation.

Memory, not history · Küchler 1988

The ceremony does not produce history — it produces memory. What the community receives is not an object to keep but an obligation to remember. The destruction that follows is not the ending of the ceremony. It is its completion.

IV
Act IV · Destruction · Transmission
The Death of the Object
The malanggan abandoned to the forest after the ceremony. Vines reclaim the surface. The decomposition is not a failure — it is the completion of the work's function.
Act IV · Stage 4 of 4

Object into obligation

The ceremony ends. The malanggan is abandoned — left to decompose, returned to the forest, the sea, or the elements. What this image shows is not destruction in the European sense. It is completion. The work has done what it was made to do.

What survives is the mental prototype — held in the memory of the right-holder, who now bears the obligation of the next actualisation. He carries the figure, invisible, until the next death requires it. The longer he carries it, the more binding the obligation, the more precisely the next figure must replicate what only he remembers.

The vines reclaiming the surface, the pigments fading, the wood returning to earth: this is the malanggan fulfilling its logic. Preservation would have been a betrayal — vital energy left undissipated, a social obligation deferred indefinitely.

Küchler 1988

"Objects that are made to be dismantled confound time not through permanence, but through renewal, and create time not as history, but as memory." The destruction does not end the work. It converts it — from object into obligation, from matter into memory. The forest completes what the ceremony began.

Since 1884, some malanggan have taken a different path — sold to European collectors, as sale was an acceptable form of removal from ritual circulation. The figure in the British Museum has survived its own death. It now exists for a gaze for which it was not made. What it transmits to that gaze is real — but it is not what it was made to transmit.
