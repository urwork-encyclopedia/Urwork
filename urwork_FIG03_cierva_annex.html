From C.1 to C.4
and beyond

How Juan de la Cierva turned four failures into one invention — and what the invention gave to the helicopter

Juan de la Cierva · Autogiro prototypes 1920–1923 · The flapping hinge · Legacy in rotary-wing aviation

Each prototype in Cierva's sequence was not a failed attempt at the same goal. It was a different question, asked more precisely. The C.1 asked whether autorotation was physically possible. The C.2 asked whether it could be controlled. The C.3 defined the exact nature of the problem. The C.4 answered it. C.1
C.1 1920 The proof of concept Failed ▼
The question it asked

Can a rotor spinning freely in the airflow — without being driven by an engine — generate enough lift to support an aircraft in forward flight? The principle of autorotation had been described theoretically, but never demonstrated on a full-scale aircraft. Cierva's C.1 was the first attempt to test it.

What failed

The C.1 used a double coaxial rotor — two superimposed rotors turning in opposite directions — intended to cancel both torque and the dissymmetry of lift. The strategy was theoretically sound but aerodynamically unworkable: the two rotors interfered with each other's airflow, generating unpredictable and asymmetrical lift patterns that made the aircraft impossible to control. Brief hops demonstrated that autorotation was physically real; sustained controlled flight was not.

What the failure taught — transmitted to C.2

Autorotation works. The C.1's failure was not a refutation of the concept but a demonstration that the concept needed refinement. The rotor did spin; lift was generated; the principle was confirmed. What remained was the problem of control and stability. The C.1 answered the foundational question and opened the next one: how do you make the rotor reliable?

C.2
C.2 1921 The stability attempt Failed ▼
The question it asked

Can the rotor be designed to produce stable, consistent lift across the full rotation? Cierva refined the blade geometry and the rotor configuration, attempting to eliminate the instability that had prevented the C.1 from achieving controlled flight. The C.2 came close to flying — close enough that Cierva believed the problem was nearly solved.

What failed

The same instability persisted. The aircraft consistently rolled to one side on takeoff, despite Cierva's modifications to the blade profile. The rolling tendency was more pronounced at lower speeds — precisely when an aircraft is most vulnerable. The C.2 revealed that the problem was not a detail of blade geometry but something more fundamental about the rotor's behaviour in forward flight.

What the failure taught — transmitted to C.3

The problem is aerodynamic, not geometric. Modifying blade shape cannot solve the instability because the instability is caused by the physics of forward flight itself: the advancing blade and the retreating blade experience different airspeeds, and therefore generate different lift regardless of their shape. This understanding — that the problem was inherent to rotation in a moving airstream — was the decisive insight that made the C.3 a more precisely targeted experiment.

C.3
C.3 1922 The problem defined Failed · decisive ▼
The question it asked

Can the dissymmetry of lift — the fundamental aerodynamic inequality between advancing and retreating blades in forward flight — be compensated by adjusting the rotor's angle of incidence? The C.3 tested a series of modifications aimed at correcting the imbalance through rotor geometry and blade pitch adjustments.

What failed — and what it revealed

All modifications failed. But observing the C.3's behaviour on the ground — the specific way the rotor rocked and the aircraft rolled — gave Cierva a precise understanding of the mechanism. The problem was not that the blades were shaped incorrectly. It was that the blades were rigid: locked into the rotor hub, they could not respond to the varying aerodynamic forces they experienced at different points in each rotation. The rigidity was the problem.

What the failure taught — transmitted to C.4

The solution is articulation, not geometry. If rigid blades cannot compensate for dissymmetry of lift, the answer is to remove the rigidity — to allow each blade to move independently in response to the aerodynamic forces acting on it. The flapping hinge was the logical conclusion of three years of precisely observed failure. Cierva had not stumbled upon it; he had deduced it by eliminating every other possibility.

C.4
C.4 17 January 1923 The flapping hinge — the solution First flight ▼
The innovation

A simple articulation — the flapping hinge — was added at the root of each rotor blade. This hinge allowed each blade to rise and fall independently as it rotated. When the advancing blade generated excess lift, it flapped upward; the resulting change in the direction of the relative airflow reduced the blade's effective angle of attack, shedding the excess lift automatically. The retreating blade flapped down, the reverse occurred. The dissymmetry of lift corrected itself continuously, without pilot input.

The first flight · Getafe, 17 January 1923

Pilot: Alejandro Gómez Spencer. The C.4 flew in stable, controlled forward flight — the first rotary-wing aircraft in history to do so. A demonstration before military authorities followed on 17 January 1923. Cierva was twenty-seven years old. He had been working on the problem for three years and four months.

The hinge — how it works

The elegance of the flapping hinge is that it does not control the dissymmetry of lift — it allows the dissymmetry to resolve itself. By permitting each blade to seek its own aerodynamic equilibrium at every point in the rotation, the hinge converts a structural problem into a dynamic equilibrium. The rotor self-regulates. This is not active control but passive compliance — a mechanical principle that requires no intelligence, no sensor, no actuator. A pivot point placed in exactly the right location.

How the solution survived the machine

The autogiro did not become the dominant rotary-wing aircraft. The helicopter, with its powered rotor and capacity for hover, eventually superseded it for most applications. But the helicopter could not have become reliable without solving the same dissymmetry-of-lift problem that Cierva had solved in 1922. Igor Sikorsky and his contemporaries adopted the flapping hinge — and variations of it — directly into helicopter rotor design. Cierva's patent, his engineering documentation, and his published analysis of the problem were the primary source.

What was lost — and what was not

The autogiro's specific advantage — inability to stall, extremely low minimum speed, safety in gusty conditions — was not transferred to the helicopter. The helicopter is more capable in many respects but not inherently safer at low speed. Cierva's original goal (an aircraft that could not fall) was achieved in the autogiro and only partially in the helicopter. The solution survived; the specific safety advantage did not fully transfer.

The open question — FIG–03

Cierva died at Croydon Airport in December 1936, killed in the crash of a KLM Douglas DC-2 — a conventional fixed-wing aircraft. He had spent seventeen years solving the problem of the stall and was killed in an aircraft that stalled. His invention — the flapping hinge — continues to operate in every articulated rotor helicopter flying today. The solution outlived the machine, and the machine outlived its inventor. What does it mean for the flapping hinge to be Cierva's most lasting work, when Cierva himself was trying to build something else entirely?
