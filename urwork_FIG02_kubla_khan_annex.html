Kubla Khan
or A Vision in a Dream

What the dream dictated, what Porlock interrupted, what waking consciousness built — S. T. Coleridge, 1797–1816

Samuel Taylor Coleridge · First published 1816, John Murray · Composed c. October 1797

Show layer

The fifty-four lines of Kubla Khan are not of a single piece. Coleridge's preface claims they arrived in a dream — but the poem's internal evidence, and a century of scholarship, suggest something more complex: a text assembled from at least three different modes of production, each with its own texture and its own kind of attention.

Dream
Images traceable to Coleridge's saturated reading (Lowes); involuntary syntax; hallucinatory density. The composition the preface claims.
Porlock
The pivot: where the poem meets its own limit. The vision fractures, turns prophetic, becomes a threshold — what the "interruption" cuts off.
Waking
First-person reflection; the poet contemplating his own inspiration from outside it; the construction of the Romantic creator as figure.
IThe Decree · lines 1–11
1
In Xanadu did Kubla KhanDream

The opening names Xanadu — Coleridge's transliteration of Shangdu, the Mongol summer capital. The source is Purchas his Pilgrimage — Coleridge cites a passage from the 1617 edition in his own preface of 1816 — specifically a passage from Book IV describing Kubla Khan's palace. It is not the last sentence of the book but the particular passage he was reading before falling asleep. The proper name arrives already sonorous, already a decree. No preparation, no approach — the poem begins in mid-authority.

2
A stately pleasure-dome decree:Dream

The verb decree is end-placed, as if the act of decreeing is itself what the line performs. Lowes traces pleasure-dome to no single source — it is a compound that the dream-work fabricated. The word stately arrives already worn smooth by reading.

3
Where Alph, the sacred river, ranDream

Alph: no river of this name exists. Lowes proposed composite origins — the Alpheus of Greek mythology (the river that flows underground and resurfaces), the Nile sources described by James Bruce, rivers in William Bartram's Travels (1791). The dream fused several rivers into one impossible name: sacred, lettered, resonant.

4
Through caverns measureless to manDream

Measureless to man — the phrase performs what it names: the caverns exceed the scale of human reckoning. Lowes found echoes in multiple travel narratives Coleridge had absorbed. The phrase has the quality of something received: its cadence is too perfect to feel invented.

5
Down to a sunless sea.Dream

The full stop after the fifth line ends the first syntactic unit — a single sentence establishing the entire geography: palace, river, caverns, a sea that receives no light. The sunless sea will be answered at line 28 by the lifeless ocean. The dream mapped the structure in advance.

6
So twice five miles of fertile groundDream

The geographic precision — twice five miles — is a direct trace of Purchas: "thus ten miles of fertile ground were inclosed within a wall." The dream has rephrased the source with almost mathematical exactness, converting prose measurement into verse without losing the number.

7
With walls and towers were girdled round:Dream

The enclosure — girdled round — sets the formal paradox of the poem's landscape: a garden of controlled pleasure within walls, with a wild chasm just beyond them. The structure of the poem echoes the structure of the domain.

8
And there were gardens bright with sinuous rills,Dream

Sinuous rills — Bartram's Travels describes the botanical richness of the American South with exactly this kind of winding, profuse water imagery. The word sinuous carries the bodily quality of a snake, of something alive and unpredictable within the ordered garden.

9
Where blossomed many an incense-bearing tree;Dream

Bartram again — aromatic trees, botanical exuberance. The garden is sensory: it can be smelled. Incense-bearing introduces a quasi-religious register that will deepen toward line 14 (as holy and enchanted).

10
And here were forests ancient as the hills,Dream

The landscape deepens in time: the forests are as old as the hills — pre-human, pre-historical. The poem is constructing an order that precedes Kubla Khan's decree, a natural world that the pleasure garden attempts to contain.

11
Enfolding sunny spots of greenery.Dream

The stanza closes on a pastoral image — sunlit clearings within the ancient forest. The word enfolding suggests shelter, almost mothering. The next line will shatter this with an abrupt exclamation: But oh! The contrast is structurally violent.

IIThe Chasm · lines 12–36
12
But oh! that deep romantic chasm which slantedDream

But oh! — the exclamation marks a rupture in the poem's composure. The garden's order is broken by the chasm. Romantic here means wild, sublime, untamed — not the literary movement. The chasm slants: it is oblique, not vertical, which gives it geological credibility and formal instability simultaneously.

13
Down the green hill athwart a cedarn cover!Dream

Cedarn — a Miltonic adjective, rare, constructed from cedar. The word signals the influence of Milton's elevated diction on Coleridge's dream-work. The cover of cedar trees makes the chasm a concealed wound in the landscape.

14
A savage place! as holy and enchantedDream

The paradox is structural: savage and holy are held in the same phrase without resolution. The chasm is both dangerous and sacred — the kind of impossible co-presence characteristic of dream logic, where contradictions coexist without needing to be resolved.

15
As e'er beneath a waning moon was hauntedDream

The waning moon — not full, not new, but diminishing — introduces the gothic-erotic atmosphere that culminates in the next line. The word haunted prepares the demon lover.

16
By woman wailing for her demon-lover!Dream

The most unguarded image in the poem: a woman wailing for a demon lover. No context, no narrative — the image arrives complete and refuses explanation. This is characteristic of dream production: the image is fully formed but entirely without justification. The demon-lover echoes the supernatural eroticism of Christabel.

17
And from this chasm, with ceaseless turmoil seething,Dream

The chasm now erupts. Ceaseless turmoil seething — the participial accumulation enacts the restlessness. This is the poem's energetic centre: the chasm is not merely a wound but a generative force, endlessly active.

18
As if this earth in fast thick pants were breathing,Dream

The earth breathes — rapidly, thickly. The simile is bodily, almost sexual: the landscape is a living body in a state of arousal or exertion. Fast thick pants is metrically odd, deliberately laboured, mimicking the breath it describes.

19
A mighty fountain momently was forced:Dream

Momently — at every moment, continuously. The fountain is not a periodic event but a constant irruption. The passive construction was forced: the fountain is compelled upward by some interior pressure, not chosen. The dream-work expresses the exact phenomenology of its own production.

20
Amid whose swift half-intermitted burstDream

Half-intermitted — partially interrupted, pulsing. The fountain's rhythm mirrors the poem's own compositional claim: not a continuous flow but a series of bursts, of which only part was captured before interruption.

21
Huge fragments vaulted like rebounding hail,Dream

Huge fragments — the word fragments in a poem that is itself a fragment is impossible to read as innocent. The erupting fountain throws up pieces of rock that do not cohere; the poem is doing the same. The simile — rebounding hail — is precise, rapid, almost violent.

22
Or chaffy grain beneath the thresher's flail:Dream

A second simile — agricultural, separating grain from chaff. The double simile (hail, then flail) is a signature of dream accumulation: the image doesn't settle on one comparison but keeps generating alternatives.

23
And 'mid these dancing rocks at once and everDream

At once and ever — simultaneously and always. The rocks dance: the geological is animated, rhythmic. The phrase compresses two temporal registers — the instantaneous and the eternal — into four syllables.

24
It flung up momently the sacred river.Dream

The chasm generates the river: the Alph is not a pre-existing watercourse but the product of the chasm's continuous eruption. Creation — the poem suggests — comes from the wound, not the garden.

25
Five miles meandering with a mazy motionDream

The alliteration — meandering… mazy motion — is almost too dense: the sound enacts the wandering it describes. Bartram's rivers in the Travels are described with exactly this quality of labyrinthine movement.

26
Through wood and dale the sacred river ran,Dream

The pastoral English landscape of the Quantock Hills where Coleridge was living bleeds into the imagined Mongol geography. Wood and dale: Somerset. Dream cartography refuses the distinction between the familiar and the invented.

27
Then reached the caverns measureless to man,Dream

The phrase caverns measureless to man returns from line 4 — a verbal rhyme across twenty-three lines. The dream-work is architecturally coherent: it built a structure that closes on itself.

28
And sank in tumult to a lifeless ocean:Dream

The river completes its cycle: born from the chasm, it sinks into the lifeless ocean — the answer to the sunless sea of line 5. The colon marks an arrival that is simultaneously an ending. The poem turns.

29
And 'mid this tumult Kubla heard from farPorlock

The pivot. Kubla Khan — the figure who has decreed and enclosed — now hears. He is no longer the agent but the listener. Something reaches him from outside the domain he has built. From far: the threat is not yet localised, not yet named. This is the hinge on which the poem's drama turns — and the place where the preface's interruption would fall.

Like the Ancient Mariner hearing the two voices above him, Kubla is receiving a signal that his enclosed world cannot contain. The poem cannot follow where this leads — and it doesn't.

30
Ancestral voices prophesying war!Porlock

The only exclamation in the poem given to a statement rather than a sight. The voices are ancestral — from the past, from the dead, from before — and their prophecy is war: the destruction of everything the pleasure-dome represents. This is the poem's impossible continuation. What happens when Kubla heeds the voices? The poem never says. The person from Porlock arrived here.

The parallel with The Rime of the Ancient Mariner is structural: in both poems, a figure encounters a vision that exceeds the moral framework available to them. In the Rime, Coleridge resorts to marginal glosses to explain what the poem cannot. In Kubla Khan, the preface serves the same function — it explains the gap rather than filling it.

31
The shadow of the dome of pleasurePorlock

The poem shifts to a shorter, more contemplative metre — as if slowing after the shock of the prophecy. The dome appears now as its own shadow, reflected on water. A reflection possesses nothing of what it depicts. The poem is already at a distance from its subject.

32
Floated midway on the waves;Porlock

Midway — not at the surface, not at the bottom, but suspended between. The dome's reflection occupies the threshold between the sunlit world above and the caverns below. The poem is suspending itself between what it has said and what it cannot say.

33
Where was heard the mingled measurePorlock

Mingled measure — the sound of the fountain above and the river below, blended. Two sources, two origins, heard together without being resolved into one. The poem is listening to itself from outside.

34
From the fountain and the caves.Porlock

The fountain (eruption, creation, the chasm's force) and the caves (enclosure, depth, the measureless underground) — the poem's two poles. They are heard together here, in the reflection, at the moment of the poem's own self-contemplation.

35
It was a miracle of rare device,Porlock

The poem steps back and names what it has been describing: a miracle of rare device. The word device — mechanism, contrivance, art — is significant. The pleasure-dome is not a natural wonder but a made thing. And so is the poem.

36
A sunny pleasure dome with caves of ice!Porlock

The poem's exclamatory summary of itself: sun and ice, pleasure and cold, the paradox held in one line. The exclamation is the boundary between the first part of the poem (third-person, geographic, dreamed) and the second (first-person, reflexive, waking). After this line, the poem changes voice completely.

IIIThe Vision and the Poet · lines 37–54
37
A damsel with a dulcimerWaking

The break. After thirty-six lines of third-person landscape, the poem shifts register entirely. A figure appears — a woman with a stringed instrument — introduced with the simplicity of a ballad. No exclamation, no geological violence. The metre shortens. We are in a different mode.

38
In a vision once I saw:Waking

I — the first person in the poem. And the vision is remembered: once I saw. This is not the dream speaking; this is someone recalling a vision from outside it. The poet has entered his own poem as a witness to his own imagination. A meta-vision: a vision of a vision.

39
It was an Abyssinian maid,Waking

Abyssinian — Ethiopia. Bruce's Travels to Discover the Source of the Nile (1790) is one of Lowes's key sources for the poem's geography. The maid comes from the same region of saturated reading as the river Alph. But here she is a figure in the poet's conscious memory, not the landscape of the dream.

40
And on her dulcimer she played,Waking

She plays — she produces music — and the poet hears it. The dulcimer is a stringed instrument struck with hammers, its sound sustained and resonant. Music as the analogue of poetic inspiration: something heard from outside oneself that the self then attempts to reproduce.

41
Singing of Mount Abora.Waking

Mount Abora — unidentified in any single source, but almost certainly derived from Milton's Paradise Lost IV, where Mount Amara is the supposed site of earthly paradise. Significantly, in the Crewe manuscript — the earliest surviving draft of the poem — Coleridge had written Amara directly, before revising it to the more fluid, sonically mysterious Abora. This revision is one of the clearest traces of the Waking layer: a deliberate, conscious adjustment of sound and allusion. The maid sings of an unreachable paradise — which is also the desire for the two hundred and fifty missing lines.

42
Could I revive within meWaking

Could I — the conditional. The poet no longer has what he once had. The vision was, it was heard, but it cannot be reliably reproduced. This is the waking mind speaking: it knows the difference between having the vision and having lost it. The dream speaks in the indicative; the waking poet speaks in the conditional.

43
Her symphony and song,Waking

The maid's music is named twice: symphony (instrumental) and song (vocal). The poet wants to revive both — the formal structure and the lyric impulse. What he is describing is the experience of trying to remember how to write the poem he has already written.

44
To such a deep delight 'twould win me,Waking

If he could revive the music, it would bring him to deep delight — a delight that is also dangerous, that leads somewhere. The word win is active: delight as conquest, as transformation.

45
That with music loud and long,Waking

The conditional unfolds: the maid's music → deep delight → music loud and long that the poet would produce. He is imagining himself as the instrument now, not the listener. The transformation from audience to maker is the waking poet's fantasy.

46
I would build that dome in air,Waking

Build that dome in air — the poem shifts from recording a vision to claiming the power to reproduce it. In air: not in stone, not in Xanadu — but in the medium of music and poetry. The poet would re-create Kubla Khan's decree, but through art rather than sovereignty.

47
That sunny dome! those caves of ice!Waking

The poem's two defining images — sun and ice — return as exclamations, pure desire. Not description but invocation. The waking poet is performing his own longing for the dream he claims to have lost.

48
And all who heard should see them there,Waking

The poem imagines its own audience: listeners who would be transformed by the music into seers. The auditory becomes visual. This is the Romantic theory of imagination as a faculty that makes the invisible visible — but here it is the poet who would produce this effect in others, not receive it himself.

49
And all should cry, Beware! Beware!Waking

The warning. The listeners, transformed, would cry Beware! The poet-figure is now dangerous — not a harmless dreamer but something that requires circumspection, ritual protection. The incantatory repetition is constructed, rhetorical — it has the quality of performance rather than vision.

50
His flashing eyes, his floating hair!Waking

The poet is described in the third person — as if seen from outside. His flashing eyes, his floating hair: the iconic image of the Romantic poet as possessed figure, visionary, quasi-divine. Coleridge is constructing a mythology of the inspired creator — a role, a costume. The waking mind is very much at work here.

51
Weave a circle round him thrice,Waking

Protective ritual — the circle woven three times to contain the dangerous figure. The imagery is folkloric, quasi-magical. It is also theatrical: the inspired poet must be separated from ordinary people by ceremony. This is not a vision but a staging.

52
And close your eyes with holy dread,Waking

Holy dread — the paradox of the sacred returns from line 14 (as holy and enchanted). What was the quality of a landscape is now the quality of the poet himself. The poem has transferred the attributes of the sublime geography onto its own creator.

53
For he on honey-dew hath fed,Waking

Honey-dew — food of the gods, the substance of visions. The poet is sustained not by ordinary food but by the diet of the inspired. This is the mythology of the creator as a being apart. The preface performs the same function: Coleridge was sustained by laudanum, by the dream, by something that could not be chosen or repeated.

54
And drunk the milk of Paradise.Waking

The final line. The milk of Paradise — not water, not wine, but milk: primal, sustaining, from the source. Paradise as a place one can drink from — and have drunk from, in the perfect tense. The poem ends in the past: hath fed, drunk. The vision is over. The last word of the poem is Paradise — and the poem is the evidence that it was reached, once, and cannot be reached again.

On the three-layer reading: These attributions are analytical propositions, not facts. The boundary between DREAM and PORLOCK (at lines 29–30) and between PORLOCK and WAKING (at line 37) cannot be proven — they are the places where the poem's internal evidence, Coleridge's preface, and a century of scholarship converge on a question rather than an answer.
